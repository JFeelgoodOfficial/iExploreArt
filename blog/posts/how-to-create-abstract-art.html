How to Create Abstract Art: A Step-by-Step Guide by JFeelgood

Abstract art is a powerful expression of emotion, colour, and form that transcends the boundaries of traditional representation. I find abstract art to be a liberating process where the rules are yours to make or break. Whether you're a seasoned artist or just starting, creating abstract work can be an exhilarating experience.

1. Start with Inspiration

Every piece of art begins with inspiration — but the source in abstract work can be anything from a fleeting emotion to a vivid dream, or even the texture of a crumpled piece of paper. I often draw from the world around me: nature, music, or a particular colour palette that catches my eye.

Tip: Keep a sketchbook or journal where you jot down ideas, colour schemes, or rough sketches. This will be your treasure trove when you sit down to create.

2. Choose Your Colours

Colour is one of the most significant elements in abstract art. The right combination can evoke emotions, create depth, and set the mood. I recommend starting with a limited palette and gradually introducing new shades as you progress.

Warm colours like red, orange, and yellow evoke passion and energy, while cool colours like blue, green, and purple can create a sense of calm and tranquility.

Tip: Experiment with small colour swatches on paper before committing to canvas. Visualise how colours interact before scaling up.

3. Embrace Spontaneity

One of the most freeing aspects of abstract art is letting go of preconceived notions and allowing spontaneity to guide your hand. Don't be afraid to make bold strokes, layer different textures, or even splatter paint.

Tip: Try unconventional tools — a palette knife, sponge, or your hands can create textures and effects that brushes may not.

4. Focus on Composition

Even in abstraction, composition plays a crucial role. The arrangement of shapes, lines, and colours guides the viewer's eye and evokes a particular response. I often start with a rough layout in mind, focusing on balance, rhythm, and contrast. Consider the rule of thirds — but don't be afraid to break it.

5. Layering and Texture

By building up layers of paint, you can add depth and complexity to your work. Each layer tells a part of the story. Texture — whether through thick impasto, subtle glazes, or mixed media — adds a tactile dimension that pulls viewers in.

Tip: Experiment with thin washes, allowing each layer to dry before adding the next. This creates a translucent effect that adds richness.

6. Know When to Stop

One of the hardest parts of abstract art is knowing when a piece is finished. For me, it's often a feeling — a sense that the piece has said all it needs to say. If you're unsure, step away for a day or two. Returning with fresh eyes will tell you everything.

"Allow yourself to explore, to play, and most importantly, to express yourself without boundaries."
— JFeelgood
